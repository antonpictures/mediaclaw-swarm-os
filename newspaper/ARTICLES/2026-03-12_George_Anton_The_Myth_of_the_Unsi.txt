The latest Asmongold reaction piece frames a briefing from Admiral Cooper’s Operation Epic Fury as proof that modern aircraft carriers are virtually impervious to attack, a narrative that has been echoed across the digital town square with claims ranging from space‑based lasers to hypersonic salvos. Yet the sentiment floating in online forums reveals a deep skepticism: users point out that even the most advanced Close‑In Weapon Systems can be overwhelmed, that unidentified submarines lurk beneath the surface, and that the sheer scale of a carrier makes it a lucrative, albeit conspicuous, target. This chorus of doubt suggests that the proclaimed invincibility is more a product of doctrinal confidence than an immutable technological reality.

Historical precedent tells a different story. During the Cold War, both the Soviet Union and China invested heavily in anti‑ship missiles, submarines, and asymmetric tactics explicitly designed to neutralize carrier battle groups. Recent exercises have demonstrated that salvoes of cruise missiles, coordinated drone swarms, and even cyber‑enabled electronic warfare can penetrate layered defenses, forcing carriers to divert resources from power projection to pure survivability. The public’s mention of “OTH recon” and “lead rope” CIWS engagements underscores that the kill chain is not a monolithic shield but a series of interdependent links, each vulnerable to disruption.  

Consequently, relying on the carrier as the cornerstone of naval power risks strategic miscalculation. While its ability to project airpower remains unmatched, the notion that it is “basically over” for any adversary ignores the evolving threat landscape and the costly implications of over‑extension. Policymakers must recalibrate force structure, investing in distributed lethality, unmanned platforms, and resilient logistics rather than betting on a single, costly hull to dominate the seas. Only by acknowledging the carrier’s limits can the United States maintain credible deterrence without courting avoidable peril.
